In a weaving village, master weaver Krishnan needed to create a silk saree border where the pattern repeated perfectly. His apprentice Meera had to find a thread count that worked for patterns of 2, 3, 4, and 6 threads without any leftover gap.

💡 Concept Check

What is the smallest thread count that is divisible by 2, 3, 4, and 6 all together?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

The answer is 12. This is the Least Common Multiple (LCM). Prime factorization: 4 = 2², 6 = 2×3. Take the highest powers: 2² × 3 = 4 × 3 = 12. So 12 is the smallest number divisible by all.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Krishnan then gave Meera a spool with 24 threads and asked her to find all the ways to arrange it in rectangular blocks without any leftover threads.

💡 Concept Check

What are all the factor pairs of 24 that Meera can use to arrange the threads?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

The factor pairs are (1, 24), (2, 12), (3, 8), and (4, 6). Factors of 24 divide it exactly. These represent possible rectangular arrangements (length × width).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Later, a new pattern required a thread count that could only be divided evenly by 1 and itself. Krishnan gave Meera a list: 13, 15, 17, 21, 23.

💡 Concept Check

Which numbers from this list (13, 15, 17, 21, 23) are prime numbers, and which are composite?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

Prime: 13, 17, 23 (they have exactly two factors: 1 and itself). Composite: 15, 21 (they have more than two factors, e.g., 15 = 3×5).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Krishnan then asked Meera to find the prime factors of 36 to understand its building blocks.

💡 Concept Check

What is the prime factorization of 36, expressed as a product of prime numbers?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

The prime factorization is 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 (or 2² × 3²). Divide step by step: 36 ÷ 2 = 18, 18 ÷ 2 = 9, 9 ÷ 3 = 3, 3 ÷ 3 = 1.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Finally, two patterns needed thread counts of 18 and 24. Krishnan wanted a single thread count that could be divided evenly by both for a combined pattern.

💡 Concept Check

What is the highest common factor (HCF) of 18 and 24, and how does it help Krishnan?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

The HCF is 6. Factors of 18: 1,2,3,6,9,18. Factors of 24: 1,2,3,4,6,8,12,24. The highest common is 6. This tells him any count that is a multiple of 6 can accommodate both.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!
