Sam was in the kitchen making lemonade. He squeezed a lemon into water, added sugar, and stirred. "Look!" Sam said. "I made lemonade. This is a change." Leo walked over, holding a piece of paper. "Well, I folded this paper into a plane. That's a change too." Uncle Raj looked up. "Those are both changes. But are they the same type? Can you get your paper back to a flat sheet, Leo? Can you get the lemon and sugar back out of the water, Sam?"

💡 Concept Check

Leo unfolded his paper plane and got a flat sheet back. Sam tried to pick the sugar out of the water but couldn't. What are the two main categories of changes called?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

The two categories are reversible changes (can be undone, like folding paper) and irreversible changes (cannot be easily undone, like making lemonade).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

To prove his point, Sam put the lemonade in the freezer. After an hour, it had turned into a solid, icy popsicle. "Now it's a solid. I can't drink it anymore," Sam said. Leo took the popsicle and put it in the microwave. It melted back into liquid lemonade. "See?" Leo said. "You changed it, and I changed it back. It's still lemonade." Uncle Raj smiled. "You just demonstrated a change where the substance changes its state but remains the same substance."

💡 Concept Check

Sam changed liquid into solid ice, and Leo changed it back to liquid. What is the general term for a change where the form of a substance changes, but its chemical identity remains the same?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

The general term is a physical change. In a physical change, properties like state (solid to liquid), shape, or size change, but it is still the exact same substance.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Uncle Raj then lit a small candle. "Watch this," he said. The wax near the flame melted into a liquid and dripped down. It hardened again. "It melted and then it got hard again," Sam said. Uncle Raj shook his head. "Look at the candle," he said, pointing to the wick. The wick was burning, getting smaller, and producing smoke and light. "The melting wax can be reversed. But can you reverse the burning of the wick? Can you put the smoke back together to make a new wick?"

💡 Concept Check

In the candle experiment, burning the wick produces ash and smoke and cannot be reversed. What is the name for a change that creates a new substance and is usually irreversible?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

The name is a chemical change. A chemical change creates entirely new substances (like smoke and ash) and cannot be easily reversed back to the original substance.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Leo blew up a balloon and let the air out. "Reversible!" he declared. Then, he accidentally knocked the burning candle into a piece of cloth. A small part of the cloth turned black and brittle. "Uh oh," Leo said, trying to rub the black spot off. The cloth tore. "I can't change this back," he said sadly. Uncle Raj put out the flame. "You've now seen both types of changes. Some changes are easy to undo; others, like burning cloth, are permanent."

💡 Concept Check

Stretching the rubber balloon is a physical change. Why is the burning of the cloth considered a chemical change?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

Burning of cloth is a chemical change because it produces new substances (ash, smoke, heat) that are completely different from the original cloth, and this change is permanent (irreversible).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

As the rain stopped, the boys realized they had spent the afternoon observing changes. They had seen reversible changes like melting, freezing, folding, and stretching. They had also seen irreversible changes like burning and cooking. "So," Uncle Raj concluded, "a change isn't just a change. Whether you can get the original substance back is the most important question. It helps us understand everything from cooking food to building materials."

💡 Concept Check

Give one example of a reversible change and one example of an irreversible change from your own daily life that were not mentioned in this story.

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

Reversible: Melting butter, boiling water, or inflating a tyre.
Irreversible: Cooking a raw egg, rusting of an iron nail, or ripening of a fruit.
