Priya and Anil were running for class president. Priya conducted a survey of 30 students and recorded their preferences in a long, messy list of names. She stared at the paper, confused about who was winning.

💡 Concept Check

How can Priya organize this raw data to easily count how many votes each candidate received?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

By creating a frequency distribution table with tally marks. This groups the data into categories and uses tally marks to easily count occurrences (groups of five).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

After counting, Priya found that she received 18 votes and Anil received 12 votes. She wanted to show this comparison visually to the class on the notice board.

💡 Concept Check

What type of graph would best show the comparison of votes between two candidates, and why?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

A bar graph. It uses bars of different heights, making it easy to visually compare quantities (18 vs 12) immediately.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Priya then wanted to show the proportion of votes each candidate received out of the total 30 votes, so students could see the "slice" of support everyone had.

💡 Concept Check

What type of graph is most suitable for showing parts of a whole, and how would Priya calculate the angle for Anil's section?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

A pie chart (circle graph). Anil's angle is calculated as (12/30) × 360° = 144°.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

After the election, Priya collected data on the favorite subjects of the same 30 students. She tallied her findings: Math: 10, Science: 8, English: 7, History: 5.

💡 Concept Check

What is the total number of students accounted for in her tally, and does this match the class size?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

Total = 10 + 8 + 7 + 5 = 30 students. Yes, it matches the class size exactly, proving her data is consistent.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Priya then wondered what would have happened if there had been three candidates instead of two, with the votes distributed as 18, 12, and 9. She wanted to know the average number of votes per candidate.

💡 Concept Check

What is the mean of these three numbers, and what does this average represent?

❌ Not quite! Math Explanation:

Mean = (18 + 12 + 9) ÷ 3 = 39 ÷ 3 = 13. This represents the central or typical value of the votes if they were evenly distributed.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!
