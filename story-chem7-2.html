Rahul watched his grandfather hammer a hot piece of iron into a horseshoe. Later, he saw an old horseshoe covered with reddish-brown flakes.

💡 Concept Check

What type of change occurs when iron is heated and hammered into a different shape, and what type of change occurs when it rusts?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

Heating and hammering iron is a physical change (only shape changes). Rusting of iron is a chemical change (new substance, iron oxide, is formed).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Rahul's grandfather then burned a piece of coal in the forge. It turned into ash and smoke, and could not be changed back.

💡 Concept Check

What evidence tells us that burning of coal is a chemical change, and why is it irreversible?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

Evidence includes release of heat/light and formation of new substances (ash/gases). It is irreversible because the original coal cannot be recovered.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

In the kitchen, Rahul's mother melted butter (which later cooled and solidified) and fried a chapati (which turned brown and crispy).

💡 Concept Check

Compare the melting of butter and the frying of a chapati. Which is a physical change and which is a chemical change?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

Melting butter is a physical change (only changes state, reversible). Frying a chapati is a chemical change (forms new substances, irreversible).

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Rahul's grandfather gave him a piece of magnesium ribbon. When held over a flame, it burned with a dazzling white light.

💡 Concept Check

What is the white powder formed when magnesium burns, and what type of change is this?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

The white powder is magnesium oxide (MgO). This is a chemical change because a new substance is formed.

✅ Excellent! +100 Points!

Later, Rahul noticed that the iron tools in the workshop were coated with oil to prevent rusting.

💡 Concept Check

What are the conditions required for rusting, and how does applying oil prevent it?

❌ Not quite! NCERT Explanation:

Rusting requires both oxygen (air) and water (moisture). Oil creates a barrier that prevents iron from contacting air and moisture.
